When 10,000 Students Hit Start at the Same Second

What really broke during the Infosys Off Campus Hiring exam outage — and what should have been there to stop it.

May 2026 ~15 min read System Design · Post-Mortem

What happened

On 17th May 2026, tens of thousands of students opened their laptops for the exam at 10:00 AM — and the portal collapsed within seconds. Not everything broke at once, and not everything broke for the same reason. Here is the exact sequence.

Figure — The incident from start to finish

Three separate failures, each with a different cause and a different fix.

Phase 1 — Login (9:30–10:00 AM): no problems

Students who logged in early had no issues. The dashboard worked, the exam card showed up, everything looked normal. This is an important clue — it means the portal itself was fine. Only the backend services behind it broke.

Phase 2 — Instruction page (10:00–10:40 AM): blank screen

At exactly 10:00 AM, everyone clicked the exam card at the same moment. The page URL changed — which means the server received the request — but nothing loaded. Just a frozen progress bar and a greyed-out Start button.

After 4–5 retries it eventually loaded for some people. Importantly, once it loaded, it worked perfectly. The problem was not the instruction page — it was the server buckling under thousands of simultaneous requests the moment the clock hit 10:00.

Phase 3 — The Start button: error every single time

After finishing the instruction steps and clicking Start, students got a branded error screen: "Internal server error." This is not a slow connection. The server replied — it just said it crashed trying to create the exam session. Students were stuck in a loop for nearly 40 minutes.

Timeout vs. HTTP 500 — quick version
A timeout = the server went quiet and never replied.
An HTTP 500 = the server replied and said "something crashed on my end."
The difference matters because a 500 tells you exactly where to look: something in the application broke, not the network.

Phase 4 — During the exam: code runner almost always failing

Even after getting in, the trouble continued. Clicking Execute on a coding question returned the same server error — instantly, not after a wait. Out of roughly 30 attempts, only about one completed successfully. Some runs got 12 of 16 test cases done, then crashed on the rest. The server was alive, but out of capacity — most requests got rejected the moment they arrived.

Figure 1 — The full incident in one view

Three rows, three independent failures: student experience (top), system collapse (middle), email disorder (bottom).

On top of all this, the post-incident communication made things worse. Students received a reschedule for "11 AM today" — at 3:30 in the afternoon. They had already received a different email at 2:30 PM saying the exam was moving to May 24. The second email arrived before the first. Both referred to times that had already passed.

Three distinct failures. Different causes. Different fixes. The rest of this post goes through each one precisely.

Failure 1 — The spike was synchronized, not just large

Imagine standing at the start line of a race. The gun fires. Everyone runs at once. Now imagine 10,000 people trying to walk through the same door at the same millisecond. That is not a traffic problem. That is a physics problem.

This is the distinction that most post-mortems miss:

High traffic vs. synchronized traffic
High traffic is thousands of students spread across 30 minutes. Five arrive per second, then ten, then twenty. Servers absorb it gradually. You can watch the curve and scale to meet it.

Synchronized traffic is those same students at exactly 10:00:00 AM. All of them hit the database in the same 200-millisecond window. There is no gradual curve. There is a wall.

Infosys's exam design created this wall on purpose — one slot time for everyone, a 30-minute window that trained students to log in the moment the clock changed.

How retrying turned a spike into a flood

The portal had a 5-second client timeout. When a request took longer than that, the browser showed an error and invited the student to try again. Every student did — immediately. Each retry was a new request added on top of requests still queued and waiting. A server already struggling under 10× its normal load now received those same requests again. Then again. The load did not stay at 10×. It compounded. The system spent all its capacity accepting requests it could never finish.

This is why the portal did not just slow down. It stayed broken for 40 minutes. Every student hitting the retry button was unknowingly making the situation worse for everyone else in the queue.

What the architecture likely looked like — and why it amplified the problem

We do not have Infosys's internal architecture diagrams. But we can make a well-grounded inference. A platform at this scale — serving thousands of concurrent exam sessions, running a proctoring layer, executing code, managing timers — almost certainly runs on a distributed, service-based architecture. The nginx reverse proxy visible at rec-test.infosys.com is consistent with multiple backend services sitting behind a single entry point. The fact that the instruction pages loaded fine while the session-start endpoint returned HTTP 500s points to separate services for separate concerns.

What the symptoms tell us is not that microservices were the wrong choice — they were almost certainly the right one at this scale. The problem was that those services were connected synchronously, without any mechanism to stop a slow service from freezing the ones above it. One click on "Start Exam" likely triggered a chain like this:

Figure 2 — One slow database froze every service above it

Without a circuit breaker, one slow database freezes the entire call chain. The failure does not stay local — it propagates upward until the browser gets nothing.

Four solutions address this failure. Each targets a different layer. Together they would have meant the exam opened on time for everyone.

Solution 1 — Virtual waiting room

The problem started before any code ran: too many students hit the backend at the same moment. The solution is to intercept that spike before it reaches origin servers — and instead of showing students an error, show them a queue position.

A virtual waiting room works exactly like a physical queue outside a concert venue. When a student arrives, they get a ticket with their position: "You are #847. Estimated wait: 4 minutes." The backend only admits as many students per second as it can comfortably handle. The exam timer does not start until they are actually inside. Students are not penalised for the wait.

Figure 3 — How a virtual waiting room meters traffic

The waiting room converts a synchronized wall of traffic into a controlled, metered stream. The backend never sees the spike. Students never see an error.

This is not custom infrastructure that needs to be built from scratch. Cloudflare offers a production-ready implementation called Cloudflare Waiting Room that can be configured in minutes. You set a threshold — say, 500 concurrent active users — and Cloudflare handles everything else: queue management, position display, session cookies, and metered admission. No backend changes required.

Solution 2 — Circuit breakers between services

Even with a waiting room, the backend can still have slow moments. A database can lag. An external API can pause. The question is: when one thing slows down, does it stay contained — or does it freeze everything connected to it?

Without a circuit breaker, every service in a synchronous call chain waits for the one below it. The Question DB slows → Question service starts timing out → it holds the API Gateway thread open → the student's browser waits the full 5 seconds → retry. With a circuit breaker, the moment Question service detects the DB is slow, it stops waiting and returns a fallback immediately. The failure stays at that one service. Session setup continues. The student gets a meaningful message, not a skeleton screen.

Figure 4 — Circuit breaker: before vs. after

The circuit breaker does not fix the slow database. It stops the slowness from propagating up the chain. The failure stays local. Everything else keeps running.

Solution 3 — Cache warming and CDN for static content

The instruction pages and exam UI are static — they look the same for every student. Serving them from the origin server during a spike is unnecessary work. A CDN (Cloudflare, Akamai, AWS CloudFront) caches these at edge nodes globally and serves them in under 50ms, regardless of how many students request them at once. The origin server is never involved.

Similarly, question data does not change during the exam. It should be loaded into a cache (Redis) 10–15 minutes before the exam starts. When students begin and the Question service gets requests, it reads from memory in under 1ms — the database is barely touched. Ticketmaster and exam platforms like HackerRank do this routinely before high-volume events.

Solution 4 — Asynchronous code execution

Running submitted code is slow — sometimes 2–10 seconds per execution, depending on the problem. If the HTTP thread sits and waits for that result, it is blocked for the entire duration. Under thousands of concurrent submissions, the thread pool drains instantly.

The fix: submit code to an internal queue, return a job ID immediately, and push the result back via WebSocket when it is ready. The student sees a spinner for a few seconds instead of an error. The server thread is freed the moment the job is submitted. This is how every competitive programming platform (Codeforces, LeetCode, HackerRank) operates — execution is always asynchronous.

The exam failure was visible in real time. The email failure was quieter but arguably more harmful — it sent contradictory information to students who were already in the middle of a stressful situation.

The root misunderstanding — ordering is not the email service's job

Before looking at the fixes, there is a conceptual point worth making clearly, because it changes how you think about the entire problem.

The right mental model
The email service's job: deliver every message, in the order it was given, to the right recipient. It does not know — and should not know — what is inside the email.

The application's job: decide which emails to send, in what order, and whether any earlier email should be cancelled before a new one goes out.

These are two completely separate responsibilities. Mix them and you get an email service that is hard to test, hard to reason about, and tightly coupled to business logic that will keep changing.

With that separation in mind, the three problems each have a clean solution at the right layer.

Fix for Problem 1 — The queue backlog

The email queue was backed up because it shared capacity with everything else — error events, retry signals, system logs. The fix is separation. Critical student-facing notifications should go on a dedicated, high-priority queue that is sized independently and never competes with system noise. A reschedule email triggered by the ops team should not queue behind 50,000 error events from the crash that caused the reschedule.

Fix for Problem 2 — Ordering (infrastructure layer, content-agnostic)

This is where the conceptual point above matters most. You do not need to write any logic that reads the email content to enforce ordering. You enforce it at the queue level, purely by routing.

The principle: route every message for the same recipient to the same queue partition, consumed by the same worker. One consumer per student = strict FIFO, automatically. The content of the email is completely irrelevant. 

# The entire ordering guarantee is this one field
producer.send(
    topic="email_notifications",
    key=user_id,        # user X → partition X → worker X → FIFO, always
    value=email_payload # worker never looks inside this to decide ordering
)

In Kafka, this is the partition key. In Amazon SQS FIFO, this is the MessageGroupId. It is a single configuration field. It does not require the queue to understand reschedules, confirmations, or any other email concept.

If you cannot change the queue infrastructure, you can enforce ordering at the consumer level using a sequence number — still without reading content: 

# At enqueue time — attach a monotonically increasing number per user
seq = redis.incr(f"email_seq:{user_id}")   # atomic — always increases
queue.send(user_id=user_id, seq=seq, payload=payload)

# At consumer time — check the number before sending
last = redis.get(f"last_sent:{user_id}") or 0
if message.seq <= last:
    return  # arrived out of order — discard silently
send_email(message.payload)
redis.set(f"last_sent:{user_id}", message.seq)

The consumer never reads the email content. It just compares two numbers. Message arriving out of order? Drop it. The email service remains completely generic.

For the most robust setup — one that also guarantees an email is never sent if the business event that triggered it failed to commit — there is the Outbox Pattern. Write to your own database table inside the same transaction as the business event. A relay process reads from that table in order, sends the email, marks it sent. Ordering lives in a SQL ORDER BY — the most reliable ordering primitive in existence. 

-- Business event and email notification in one atomic write
BEGIN;
  UPDATE exam_sessions SET status = 'rescheduled' WHERE student_id = 42;
  INSERT INTO outbox (user_id, payload, created_at, sent_at)
  VALUES (42, '{"template":"reschedule"}', NOW(), NULL);
COMMIT;

-- Relay reads in strict order — regardless of how many workers run
SELECT * FROM outbox
WHERE  sent_at IS NULL
ORDER BY user_id, created_at ASC;

Fix for Problem 3 — Supersession (application layer)

This one is the application's responsibility — and only the application's. The email service must not know about it. When the ops team triggered Event B, they should have cancelled Event A before enqueuing anything. The email service only sees status = 'pending' rows. It executes what it is given, in order, and nothing more. 

-- Cancel what is no longer true. Then create what is.
BEGIN;
  UPDATE notification_jobs
  SET    status = 'cancelled'
  WHERE  student_id = 42 AND status = 'pending';

  INSERT INTO notification_jobs (student_id, template, status)
  VALUES (42, 'reschedule_may24', 'pending');
COMMIT;

The email service never sees the cancelled row. It does not know a reschedule happened. It does not need to. The business decision was made in the application. The email service just delivers the remaining truth.

The key takeaway
A standard queue is a throughput primitive, not an ordering primitive. It promises: "I will deliver every message at least once." It does not promise: "in the order you gave them to me." If you need both — delivery and order — you need to buy that guarantee explicitly, at the infrastructure level, before any message enters the pipe. The content of the message is irrelevant to this decision. Structure is everything. Content is nothing.

What to remember

01
High traffic and synchronized traffic are different problems

Your capacity number is a rate — requests per second, sustained. A thundering herd delivers the entire burst in milliseconds. If you have not simulated everyone hitting start at the same second, you have not tested for the actual risk.

02
Microservices do not protect you — circuit breakers do

Independent deployability is not the same as independent failure. Without circuit breakers, one slow database freezes every service in the chain. Microservices actually make this worse, because there are more links in the chain. The architecture style does not matter. The resilience patterns do.

03
A fast rejection is better than a slow timeout

A request rejected in 1ms uses almost no resources. A request that waits 5 seconds and then retries holds connection slots, threads, and memory — and then doubles the load on the next attempt. Saying no quickly often delivers a better experience than trying to say yes to everyone at once.

04
A standard queue promises delivery, not order

At-least-once delivery and FIFO are two separate guarantees. If you need both, you need the right tool — Kafka partition keys, SQS FIFO groups, or the outbox pattern. The default behaviour is: deliver everything, in whatever order workers happen to finish.

05
The email service should not understand what it is sending

Ordering and cancellation logic belongs in the application layer, handled before any message enters the email pipeline. A well-designed email service is a generic, reliable pipe. Keep it that way.

06
Internal tools deserve the same testing as public products

This exam is used by lakhs of students at a moment that directly shapes their careers. The word "internal" does not reduce the obligation to test it under realistic load. The people on the other side of the screen do not know — or care — that it is an internal tool.
